Chapter I

Navier-Stokes Equations — Fluid Mechanics

1. Conservation Laws (Derivation Starting Point)

Everything starts from two integral conservation laws applied to a material control volume V(t):

Mass Conservation
∂ρ/∂t + ∇·(ρu) = 0
Continuity equation. For incompressible flow: ∇·u = 0 (divergence-free velocity field).
Momentum Conservation
ρ Du/Dt = ∇·σ + ρf
σ = Cauchy stress tensor. f = body force per unit mass (e.g. gravity). D/Dt = material derivative.

2. Newtonian Constitutive Relation & Full N-S Equation

Stress Decomposition
σ = −pI + τ
p = thermodynamic pressure; τ = viscous stress tensor.
Newtonian Viscous Stress
τ = 2μS + λ(∇·u)I
S = ½(∇u + (∇u)ᵀ) = strain-rate tensor. μ = dynamic viscosity, λ = second viscosity.
Full Navier-Stokes
ρ Du/Dt = −∇p + μ∇²u + (μ+λ)∇(∇·u) + ρf
General form for a compressible Newtonian fluid with constant μ, λ.
Incompressible N-S
ρ(∂u/∂t + u·∇u) = −∇p + μ∇²u + ρf
The most-used form: ∇·u = 0 eliminates the compressibility term. This is what most exercises use.

3. Energy Conservation & Bernoulli Equation

Taking the scalar product of the incompressible N-S momentum equation with u and manipulating identities gives the kinetic energy equation. In the inviscid, steady-state, incompressible limit with a conservative body force f = −∇Φ:

Bernoulli (along a streamline)
p + ½ρ|u|² + ρgz = const
Valid for: inviscid (μ=0), steady, incompressible, conservative body forces. Used for pipes, nozzles, manometers.
Viscous Dissipation
τ:∇u = 2μ S:S ≥ 0
Always positive for Newtonian fluids — irreversible conversion of mechanical energy to heat.

4. Boundary & Initial Conditions

  • No-slip: u = u_wall on solid surfaces (most common in exercises).
  • Initial condition: u(x, 0) = u₀(x) for time-dependent problems.
  • Traction (open boundary): σ·n = T on inflow/outflow surfaces.
  • Periodic or far-field: used in channel flow / external flow problems.

5. Nondimensionalization & Reynolds Number

Choose characteristic length L and velocity U. The dimensionless incompressible N-S becomes:

Dimensionless N-S
∂u/∂t + u·∇u = −∇p + (1/Re)∇²u + f*
All variables are now dimensionless. Re governs the ratio of inertia to viscosity.
Reynolds Number
Re = ρUL / μ
Re ≪ 1 → viscous (laminar, Stokes flow). Re ≫ 1 → inertia-dominated (turbulent). Darcy's Law valid for Re < 1.

6. Classical Exact Solutions (must know)

Plane Couette Flow — shear-driven

Two parallel plates at y=0 (stationary) and y=h (moving at speed U). Governing equation: 0 = μ d²u/dy² with u(0)=0, u(h)=U.

Couette Velocity Profile
u(y) = (U/h) · y
Linear profile. No pressure gradient needed. Shear stress τ = μU/h = constant throughout.

Plane Poiseuille Flow — pressure-driven

Two fixed plates. Constant pressure gradient −dp/dx drives the flow. Governing equation: 0 = −dp/dx + μ d²u/dy².

Poiseuille Velocity Profile
u(y) = (1/2μ)(−dp/dx) · y(h−y)
Parabolic profile. Maximum at centerline y=h/2: u_max = (h²/8μ)(−dp/dx). Mean velocity = (2/3) u_max.
Strategy for N-S exercises: (1) Identify what drives the flow — shear or pressure gradient? (2) Choose which terms vanish (steady → ∂/∂t=0; fully developed → u·∇u=0; no body force → f=0). (3) Integrate twice; apply boundary conditions to find integration constants. (4) Check energy: does Bernoulli apply? (inviscid + steady + streamline).
Exercise · Navier-Stokes — Building Pressure
A drinking-water pipeline has pressure 2.758 bar on the ground floor. What is the maximum height the water can reach?
Solution — Bernoulli (static fluid, u=0)
  1. Bernoulli: p₁ + ρgz₁ = p₂ + ρgz₂. At max height, pressure = p_atm = 1.013×10⁵ Pa and u=0.
  2. p₁ = 2.758 bar = 2.758×10⁵ Pa; p₂ = 1.013×10⁵ Pa; Δp = 1.745×10⁵ Pa.
  3. h = Δp / (ρg) = 1.745×10⁵ / (1000 × 9.81) ≈ 17.8 m
Exercise · Venturi Meter
Water at 20°C flows through a Venturi tube. Pressure at throat is 2 atm below inlet pressure. Inlet velocity = 0.6 m/s. Find throat velocity.
Solution — Bernoulli + Continuity
  1. Bernoulli between inlet (1) and throat (2): p₁ + ½ρv₁² = p₂ + ½ρv₂²
  2. Rearrange: ½ρ(v₂²−v₁²) = p₁−p₂ = 2 atm = 2×101325 = 202650 Pa
  3. v₂² = 2×202650/1000 + (0.6)² = 405.30 + 0.36 = 405.66 → v₂ ≈ 20.14 m/s

Sedimentary Geology & Petroleum Systems

1. Rock Classification

TypeFormationSub-typesRelevance
IgneousCooling of magma/lavaIntrusive (granite), Extrusive (basalt)<1% of petroleum
SedimentaryCompaction of sedimentsClastic (sandstone), Chemical (limestone), Organic (coal)~99% of petroleum
MetamorphicHeat + pressure without meltingFoliated (schist), Non-foliated (marble)Rare, by migration only

Why sedimentary dominates: Sedimentary environments allow organic matter to accumulate, be preserved, and undergo thermal transformation into hydrocarbons over millions of years.

2. The Petroleum System — Four Essential Elements

1. Source Rock
Kerogen → Heat → Oil/Gas
Organic-rich sedimentary rock (shale, mudstone, coal). Thermal maturation of kerogen at depth generates hydrocarbons. Key metric: Total Organic Carbon (TOC).
2. Reservoir Rock
High φ + High k
Porous and permeable rock storing hydrocarbons after migration. Examples: sandstone, limestone, dolomite. Characterized by porosity φ and permeability k.
3. Seal / Cap Rock
k ≈ 0, impermeable
Overlying impermeable layer (shale, salt, anhydrite) preventing upward hydrocarbon leakage. Structural integrity is critical.
4. Trap
Closed geometry
Structural traps (anticlines, faults, domes) or stratigraphic traps (pinch-outs, reefs, unconformities). Prevents further migration and allows economic accumulation.

3. Petroleum System Processes

ProcessDefinitionMechanism / Driving Force
GenerationConversion of organic matter → hydrocarbonsThermal maturation of kerogen; burial depth, temperature history, geological time
MigrationMovement from source → reservoirPressure gradients, buoyancy (oil/gas less dense than water), rock compaction — primary (short range) then secondary (through permeable pathways/faults)
AccumulationConcentration in reservoirBuoyancy-driven upward migration blocked by seal; requires porous reservoir + effective trap geometry
PreservationLong-term retentionSeal continuity, basin stability, limited faulting/erosion, minimal uplift
Key Rule: All four elements AND all four processes must be present and properly timed geologically. Missing any one element means no petroleum accumulation.

4. Sedimentary Basins

  • Regions of long-term subsidence where sediments accumulate (Paleozoic → Cenozoic).
  • Formed by tectonic compression or extension; classified by Paleozoic (540 MYA), Mesozoic (250 MYA), Cenozoic (65 MYA) eras.
  • Reservoir structure types along continental slopes: by-pass channels, confined channel complexes, weakly-confined complexes, lobes.

  • Seismic reflection: 2D/3D/4D surveys detect subsurface structure by measuring reflected acoustic waves.
  • Basin analysis & petroleum system modeling: Reconstructs burial history, heat flow, and maturation timing.
  • Risk assessment: Evaluates probability of the four elements being simultaneously present.

3. Reservoir Engineering — Core Parameters

Key Parameters

Porosity φ, Permeability k in millidarcies, Pressure P, Temperature T, Fluid properties via PVT analysis.
Material Balance
N_p = f(ΔP_reservoir)
Cumulative oil production Nₚ as a function of reservoir pressure decline. Goal: maximize recovery factor while maintaining economic viability.

Flow Assurance Issues (Midstream)

  • Wax deposition: Paraffin waxes precipitate as crude cools.
  • Corrosion: H₂S and CO₂ attack steel pipelines.
Chapter IV — Part A

Porous Rocks & Fluid Properties

1. Fundamental Definitions

Continuum Hypothesis (REV): We treat the complex pore network as an average medium at a macroscopic scale. The Representative Elementary Volume (REV) must be: V ≫ pore scale AND V ≪ domain scale — so that measured properties become scale-invariant.
Total (Geometric) Porosity
φ = Vₚ / Vᵧ = Vₚ / (Vₚ + Vₛ)
Dimensionless, 0 < φ < 1. Typical: sandstone ≈ 0.20, soil ≈ 0.30, granite ≈ 0.05. NOT sufficient alone to describe flow — need connectivity.
Effective (Connected) Porosity
ε = Vₚ꜀ / Vₜ = φ · η
Only interconnected pores contribute to flow. η = Vf/Vp = saturation coefficient. Flow depends on connected pores, not total pores.
Void Ratio (Soil Mechanics)
e = Vₚ / Vₛ = φ / (1 − φ)
Common in geotechnical engineering. Related to porosity: φ = e/(1+e).
Specific Surface Area
a = Aₚ / Vᵧ [m⁻¹]
Pore surface per unit bulk volume. Important for adsorption and catalytic reactions. For spheres: Sv = 3(1−φ)/r.
Tortuosity
τ = (Lₑ / L)² ≥ 1
Accounts for winding pore paths. Lₑ = effective path length, L = straight-line length. Reduces effective permeability.
Intrinsic Permeability
k = −μ Uᴅ / ∇P [m²]
Property of pore geometry only — independent of fluid. 1 Darcy ≈ 10⁻¹² m². Sandstone: 0.1–1000 mD; Shale: 10⁻⁵–10 mD; Clean sand: 1000–10,000 mD.
Hydraulic Conductivity
K = k · ρ_f · g / μ [m/s]
Combines pore geometry AND fluid properties. Used in hydrology. K = k·ρg/μ ties to Darcy velocity via q = −K∇h.
Transmissibility
T = k · A / (μ · L)
Lumped parameter combining permeability, geometry, and fluid viscosity. Units: m³/(Pa·s) or m³·s/kg.

2. Porous Media Types

3. Porosity from Density Data

Given bulk density ρ_bulk and grain density ρ_grain:

Porosity from Density
φ = 1 − ρ_bulk / ρ_grain
Also: Vs = Mass/ρs; Vp = VT − Vs; φ = Vp/VT. Standard approach in lab measurements.
Exercise I — Porosity & Void Ratio
Dry soil sample: total volume Vᵧ = 500 cm³, mass = 800 g, grain density ρₛ = 2.65 g/cm³. Calculate φ and void ratio e.
Solution (from slides, exact values)
  1. Volume of solids: Vₛ = 800 / 2.65 ≈ 301.89 cm³
  2. Pore volume: Vₚ = 500 − 301.89 = 198.11 cm³
  3. Porosity: φ = 198.11 / 500 = 0.396 (39.6%)
  4. Void ratio: e = 198.11 / 301.89 = 0.656
Exercise I (Exercises sheet) — Sandstone Core
Sandstone core plug: bulk volume Vb = 25.0 cm³. Mercury porosimetry: connected pore volume = 5.5 cm³, isolated pore volume = 0.7 cm³. Calculate φT, φe, and φe/φT.
Solution
  1. Total porosity: φT = (5.5 + 0.7)/25.0 = 6.2/25.0 = 0.248 (24.8%)
  2. Effective porosity: φe = 5.5/25.0 = 0.220 (22.0%)
  3. Ratio: φe/φT = 5.5/6.2 = 0.887 — 88.7% of pores are connected. Effective porosity is what matters for flow.
Exercise II — Granular Pack
Spheres of radius r = 0.15 mm; ρ_bulk = 1.68 g/cm³; ρ_grain = 2.65 g/cm³. (1) Compute φ. (2) Derive Sv in terms of r and φ. Evaluate numerically. (3) Hydraulic radius rh = φ/Sv — calculate and compare to r.
Solution
  1. φ = 1 − 1.68/2.65 = 1 − 0.634 = 0.366 (36.6%)
  2. For a pack of spheres: Sv = (surface of spheres per unit bulk volume) = 3(1−φ)/r = 3×0.634/0.15×10⁻³ = 12,680 m⁻¹
  3. rh = φ/Sv = 0.366/12680 = 2.89×10⁻⁵ m = 0.029 mm ≈ r/5.2. The hydraulic radius is about 5× smaller than grain radius — flow channels are narrower than grain size.
Chapter IV — Part B

Darcy's Law & Flow in Porous Media

1. Darcy's Experiment (1856)

Henry Darcy's sand-filter experiment measured: flow rate Q, cross-sectional area A, head difference Δh = h₁ − h₂, length L. He found empirically:

Darcy's Empirical Law
Q = K · A · (Δh / L)
Q proportional to A, to Δh, and inversely to L. K = hydraulic conductivity [m/s]. Valid for laminar, low Re flow.

2. Darcy's Law — General Formulations

Darcy Velocity (specific discharge)
q = Q/A = −K · (Δh/ΔL)
q [m/s] is NOT the real fluid velocity. It is flow per unit total cross-sectional area, including solid phase.
In Terms of Pressure
q = −(k/μ)∇p = −K∇(p + ρ_f gz)
Full form including gravity. The hydraulic head h = p/(ρg) + z combines pressure and elevation heads.
1D with Gravity (vertical)
q = −(k/μ)(dp/dl + ρg/(1.0133×10⁶) · dz/dl)
Form used in petroleum engineering with Darcy units. The factor 1.0133×10⁶ converts atm to dyn/cm² (cgs system).
Interstitial (Actual) Velocity
v = q / φ
Real average fluid velocity through connected pore space. Always v > q since φ < 1. Critical distinction in transport problems.
IMPORTANT: Darcy velocity q is NOT the actual fluid velocity. The actual interstitial velocity is v = q/φ, which is always larger. This distinction matters in tracer/contaminant transport calculations.

3. Validity & Limitations of Darcy's Law

  • Valid only in the laminar regime (Re < ~1 based on grain size).
  • Gives a linear relationship between flux and pressure gradient.
  • Breaks down at high velocities → Forchheimer equation adds a quadratic inertia term.
  • Not applicable to gas flow at very low pressures (Klinkenberg effect).

4. Unit Systems — Darcy Units

Dimensional analysis of Darcy's Law u = −(k/μ)(dΦ/dl) in absolute cgs units gives:

Permeability Unit
1 Darcy ≈ 10⁻⁸ cm² = 10⁻¹² m²
Conversions: 1 atm = 1.0133×10⁶ dyn/cm²; 1 cp = 10⁻² P. 1 Darcy: flow of 1 cp fluid at 1 cm/s under 1 atm/cm gradient through 1 cm² area.
Field Unit Form (petroleum)
q(stb/d) = 1.127×10⁻³ k(mD)·A(ft²)·Δp(psi) / [μ(cp)·Bo·L(ft)]
Bo = formation volume factor [rb/stb]. Used directly in reservoir engineering calculations.

5. Micro-Macro Link — Poiseuille vs Darcy

Treating the porous medium as a bundle of capillary tubes of radius R:

Poiseuille (microscale)
v_avg = (R²ρg / 8μ) · (Δh/L)
Average velocity in a single capillary tube — derived from N-S with no-slip. This is the pore-scale foundation of Darcy's Law.
Permeability from Pore Size
k = φR² / (8τ)
k ∝ R² — doubling pore diameter quadruples permeability! Tortuosity τ reduces k. This is the Carman-Kozeny model linkage.

6. Kozeny-Carman Equation

Kozeny-Carman
k = φ³ / (τ · C · Sv²)
C = Kozeny constant (≈ 2 for circular tubes). Sv = specific surface area [m⁻¹]. Connects pore geometry to permeability. Used to estimate k from φ and grain size.

7. Layered Systems — Equivalent Permeability

Perpendicular Flow (Series)
L/k_eq = L₁/k₁ + L₂/k₂ + …
Harmonic mean, weighted by thickness. Dominated by the LOW-permeability layer. Analogous to resistors in series.
Parallel Flow
k_eq = (k₁L₁ + k₂L₂ + …) / (L₁+L₂+…)
Arithmetic mean, weighted by thickness. Dominated by the HIGH-permeability layer. Analogous to resistors in parallel.
Exercise 1 — Simple Darcy (slides)
Vertical sandstone column L=1m, k=10⁻¹¹ m², μ=10⁻³ Pa·s (water at 20°C), ρ=1000 kg/m³, g=9.81 m/s², Δp=1000 Pa. Find K, q, and Q for A=0.01 m².
Solution (exact from slides)
  1. K = kρg/μ = 10⁻¹¹ × 1000 × 9.81 / 10⁻³ = 9.81 × 10⁻⁵ m/s
  2. q = −(k/μ)(Δp/L) = 9.81×10⁻⁵ × 1000/1 = 0.0981 m/s
  3. Q = q × A = 0.0981 × 0.01 = 9.81 × 10⁻⁴ m³/s ≈ 0.98 L/s
Exercise 2 — Horizontal Pipe (slides)
Horizontal pipe, A=10 cm²=10⁻³ m², K=10⁻³ m/s, pressure drop = 2 m of water over L=1 m. Find Q.
Solution
  1. Hydraulic gradient: Δh/L = 2/1 = 2
  2. Q = K·A·(Δh/L) = 10⁻³ × 10⁻³ × 2 = 2 × 10⁻⁶ m³/s = 2 cm³/s
Exercise 3 — Layered Reservoir (slides)
Two layers in series: Layer A (L₁=5m, k₁=100 mD), Layer B (L₂=10m, k₂=10 mD). Find equivalent permeability for (a) perpendicular flow and (b) parallel flow.
Solution (exact from slides)
  1. Perpendicular (series): k_eq = 15/(5/100 + 10/10) = 15/(0.05 + 1.0) = 15/1.05 = 14.29 mD — dominated by low-k layer B.
  2. Parallel: k_eq = (100×5 + 10×10)/15 = (500+100)/15 = 600/15 = 40 mD — dominated by high-k layer A.
Exercise IV (Exercises sheet) — Darcy Units
Water (μ=1.00 cp, ρ=1.00 g/cm³) flows horizontally through sandpack: L=50 cm, A=12 cm², p₁=3.50 atm, p₂=1.00 atm, k=0.250 Darcy. (a) Calculate q in cm³/s. (b) Convert to field units (stb/d).
Solution
  1. In Darcy units: u = k/μ × Δp/L = 0.250/1.00 × (3.50−1.00)/50 = 0.250 × 0.05 = 0.01250 cm/s
  2. q = u × A = 0.01250 × 12 = 0.150 cm³/s
  3. To stb/d: 0.150 cm³/s × 86400 s/d ÷ 158,987 cm³/bbl × (1/Bo) → with Bo≈1: ≈ 0.0814 stb/d
Exercise V — Vertical Core with Gravity
Vertical core plug (length 5 cm, diameter 2.54 cm), oil: μ=3.5 cp, ρ=0.82 g/cm³, k=150 mD. Flow directed downward; +z upward. p_top=2.00 atm, p_bottom=1.50 atm. (a) Write full Darcy velocity including gravity. (b) Calculate u and q. (c) Does gravity aid or oppose pressure-driven flow?
Solution
  1. Full expression: u = −(k/μ)[dp/dl + ρg/(1.0133×10⁶) · dz/dl]
  2. Take downward as positive flow direction (dl is downward, dz/dl = −1 since z increases upward).
  3. dp/dl = (p_bottom − p_top)/L = (1.50−2.00)/5 = −0.10 atm/cm (pressure increases upward, decreases downward)
  4. Gravity term: ρg/(1.0133×10⁶) × (−1) = −0.82×980.7/(1.0133×10⁶) = −7.94×10⁻⁴ atm/cm
  5. u = −(150×10⁻³/3.5)×[−0.10 − 7.94×10⁻⁴] ≈ −(0.04286)×(−0.10079) ≈ +0.00432 cm/s (downward)
  6. A = π(1.27)² = 5.067 cm²; q = 0.00432 × 5.067 ≈ 0.0219 cm³/s
  7. Gravity aids the flow (both pressure gradient and gravity act downward).
Exercise VII — Field Unit Problem
Field engineer measures q=350 stb/d of crude oil (μ=5.0 cp, Bo=1.15 rb/stb) through horizontal reservoir: w=200 ft, h=30 ft, L=800 ft, Δp=120 psi. Using field-unit Darcy Law: q=1.127×10⁻³ k·A·Δp(psi)/[μ·Bo·L]. (a) Solve for k in mD. (b) With k=180 mD, find revised q.
Solution
  1. A = 200×30 = 6000 ft²
  2. k = q·μ·Bo·L / (1.127×10⁻³ × A × Δp) = 350 × 5.0 × 1.15 × 800 / (1.127×10⁻³ × 6000 × 120)
  3. k = 1,610,000 / 811.44 ≈ 1984 mD ≈ 1.98 D
  4. Revised q with k=180 mD: q = 1.127×10⁻³ × 180 × 6000 × 120 / (5.0 × 1.15 × 800) = 145,965.6 / 4600 ≈ 31.7 stb/d
Chapter V

Flow in Inhomogeneous Porous Media (Numerical DE)

1. Governing Equation for Heterogeneous Permeability

For incompressible flow through a 2D domain Ω = [0,Lx]×[0,Ly] with spatially varying permeability k(x,y) = k₀ e^(αx+βy):

General Flow Equation
∇·(K∇p) = 0
Derived from Darcy (q=−K∇p) + incompressibility (∇·q=0). K = k(x,y)/μ. This is the governing PDE to solve.
Simplified (exponential k)
∇²p + α∂p/∂x + β∂p/∂y = 0
When k = k₀e^(αx+βy), taking ∇·(K∇p)=0 and dividing by K gives this PDE. α,β are spatial variation constants.

2. Analytical Solution via Separation of Variables

Assume separable solution p(x,y) = X(x)·Y(y). Substituting into ∇²p + α∂p/∂x + β∂p/∂y = 0:

Strategy — Separation of Variables
  1. Write p(x,y) = X(x)·Y(y).
  2. Divide everything by X·Y to get: X''/X + αX'/X + Y''/Y + βY'/Y = 0
  3. Set x-dependent part = −λ² and y-dependent part = +λ² (separation constant).
  4. X-ODE: X'' + αX' + λ²X = 0 → characteristic roots: r = [−α ± √(α²−4λ²)]/2
  5. Y-ODE: Y'' + βY' − λ²Y = 0 → characteristic roots: s = [−β ± √(β²+4λ²)]/2
  6. Apply boundary conditions: p(0,y)=p₀, p(Lx,y)=p₁, no-flow at y=0 and y=Ly (∂p/∂y=0).
  7. No-flow BC forces Y'(0) = Y'(Ly) = 0 → restricts λ to discrete values: λn = nπ/Ly.
  8. For n=0 (uniform in y): X'' + αX' = 0 → X(x) = C₁ + C₂e^(−αx). Apply pressure BCs to get the 1D solution.
1D Analytical Solution (α≠0)
p(x) = p₀ + (p₁−p₀)(1−e^(−αx))/(1−e^(−αLx))
Valid when α,β≠0 and the flow is essentially 1D (no-flow lateral BCs). For α=β=0: linear pressure profile p(x)=p₀+(p₁−p₀)x/Lx.
Velocity Field
v(x,y) = −(k(x,y)/μ)∇p
Compute after finding p analytically or numerically. Permeability heterogeneity (α,β≠0) bends flow paths away from straight lines.

3. Numerical Solution — Finite Difference Method

Strategy — FDM Discretization of ∇·(K∇p) = 0
  1. Grid: Discretize domain into Nx×Ny cells with spacing Δx, Δy. Node (i,j) at (iΔx, jΔy).
  2. Finite difference approximation: At node (i,j):
    [K_{i+½}(p_{i+1,j}−p_{i,j}) − K_{i-½}(p_{i,j}−p_{i-1,j})]/Δx² + [similar in y] = 0
  3. Interface permeability: Use harmonic mean: K_{i+½} = 2K_i·K_{i+1}/(K_i+K_{i+1}) to preserve flux continuity.
  4. Assembly: Write as linear system Ap = b where A is sparse (pentadiagonal in 2D).
  5. Boundary conditions: Dirichlet (fixed p) → set row to identity + RHS. Neumann (no-flow) → ghost nodes or one-sided difference.
  6. Solve: Use direct solver (LU) for small grids; iterative (conjugate gradient, multigrid) for large grids.
  7. Validate: Compare numerical p(x,y) to analytical solution; compute L∞ and L₂ errors; check convergence as Δx→0.

4. Key Physical Questions

QuestionAnswer
How does permeability heterogeneity affect flow paths?High-k zones attract streamlines (preferential flow paths); low-k zones deflect them. Exponential k creates curved, non-uniform flux even with parallel BCs.
When does flow become purely 1D?When β=0 (no y-variation in k) AND no-flow BCs on top/bottom: pressure becomes uniform in y direction, reducing to 1D flow in x.
Special case α=β=0?Uniform permeability → ∇²p=0 (Laplace equation) → linear pressure profile, uniform velocity field.

Exercise III — Tortuosity & Kozeny-Carman
Tortuosity τ=(Le/L)². Tracer experiment on 10 cm sandpack gives mean transit path length 14.3 cm. (a) Find τ. (b) Using k=φ³/(τCSv²) with C=2, φ=0.30, Sv=1.2×10⁴ m⁻¹, estimate k in m² and convert to millidarcies.
Solution
  1. τ = (Le/L)² = (14.3/10)² = (1.43)² = 2.045
  2. k = φ³/(τ·C·Sv²) = (0.30)³ / (2.045 × 2 × (1.2×10⁴)²)
  3. k = 0.027 / (2.045 × 2 × 1.44×10⁸) = 0.027 / (5.89×10⁸) = 4.58×10⁻¹¹ m²
  4. In millidarcies: 4.58×10⁻¹¹ / 10⁻¹⁵ = 4.58×10⁴ mD = 45,800 mD ≈ 45.8 Darcy (clean sand range — reasonable)
Summary

Master Strategies for Each Problem Type

Strategy A — Navier-Stokes / Fluid Statics Problems

Step-by-Step Approach
  1. Identify the regime: Static? Steady? Inviscid? Incompressible?
  2. Choose the right equation: Static → hydrostatics (p = ρgh + const). Inviscid + steady → Bernoulli. Viscous + steady, fully developed → Reduced N-S (ODE).
  3. For Bernoulli problems: Identify two points on the same streamline, write p₁+½ρv₁²+ρgz₁ = p₂+½ρv₂²+ρgz₂. Don't forget: both must be on the same streamline, flow must be inviscid and steady.
  4. For manometer/pressure problems: Work from known pressure, going through fluid layers, adding ρgh going down and subtracting going up.
  5. For channel flows (Poiseuille/Couette): Start from N-S, strike out ∂/∂t, u·∇u (fully developed), keep only d²u/dy², integrate twice, apply no-slip BCs.
  6. Check units before finalizing — especially atm vs Pa, bar vs Pa.

Strategy B — Porosity / Permeability Problems

Step-by-Step Approach
  1. Porosity from volumes: Identify VT (bulk), Vs (solid), Vp = VT − Vs. Then φ = Vp/VT. If mass and density given: Vs = m/ρs.
  2. Effective vs total porosity: φT includes isolated pores; φe only connected pores. Only φe matters for flow. For mercury porosimetry: φe = connected pore vol / bulk vol.
  3. Permeability from Kozeny-Carman: k = φ³/(τ·C·Sv²). Calculate Sv from geometry (for spheres: Sv = 3(1−φ)/r). Calculate τ from tracer experiment: τ=(Le/L)².
  4. Convert units: 1 Darcy = 10⁻¹² m²; 1 mD = 10⁻¹⁵ m².

Strategy C — Darcy's Law Problems

Step-by-Step Approach
  1. Identify geometry: 1D horizontal? Vertical (with gravity)? Layered?
  2. Choose unit system: SI (Pa, m, m²) vs Darcy units (atm, cm, Darcy, cp) vs field units (psi, ft, mD, cp, stb/d).
  3. For horizontal 1D: q = (k/μ)·(Δp/L); Q = q·A. Simple and direct.
  4. For vertical flow (gravity matters): Use full form with gravity term. Determine sign: is gravity aiding or opposing the pressure-driven flow? Check direction of dz/dl relative to flow direction.
  5. For layered media: Perpendicular → harmonic mean (series); Parallel → arithmetic mean (weighted by thickness).
  6. Don't confuse q and v: q = Darcy velocity (per total area); v = q/φ = actual pore velocity.
  7. For field units: Use the formula q(stb/d) = 1.127×10⁻³·k(mD)·A(ft²)·Δp(psi)/[μ(cp)·Bo·L(ft)].

Strategy D — Heterogeneous Flow / Numerical PDE Problems

Step-by-Step Approach
  1. Derive governing PDE: Start from ∇·q=0 and q=−(k/μ)∇p → ∇·(K∇p)=0 where K=k/μ.
  2. Simplify if k is exponential: k=k₀e^(αx+βy) → divide by K → ∇²p + α∂p/∂x + β∂p/∂y = 0.
  3. Analytical route: Assume p=X(x)·Y(y), substitute, separate into two ODEs, solve each ODE (characteristic equation), apply BCs.
  4. Numerical route (FDM): Discretize domain, use harmonic mean for interface K, assemble sparse linear system Ap=b, apply Dirichlet (pressure) and Neumann (no-flow) BCs, solve, validate vs. analytical.
  5. Compute velocity field: v = −(K/μ)∇p using finite differences on the solved pressure field.
  6. Answer qualitative questions: α,β=0 → homogeneous → 1D linear. β=0 only → 1D but non-linear. α,β≠0 → 2D curved flow paths.
